Shoe Contamination Comparison: New York City vs. New Delhi

Disgustometer Scale: 1 = Lowest Risk / Cleanest, 10 = Highest Risk / Most Disgusting

Dimension NYC
(Disgustometer Score)		 New Delhi
(Disgustometer Score)
1. Probability of Stepping on Feces
(human or animal waste)		 5/10[1]
Dog waste is a known issue on many sidewalks, despite “pooper scooper” laws. Rat droppings in alleyways also contribute. Human feces is rare but reported in some areas. 		9/10[2]
High stray dog populations, open defecation in some areas, and cattle on streets mean feces are frequently encountered—often unavoidable.
2. Likelihood of Contact with Other Organic Waste
(food scraps, rotting garbage, etc.)		 4/10[3]
NYC’s curbside trash bags can leak organic waste, especially in warm months. However, regular collection somewhat limits prolonged exposure on sidewalks. 		8/10[4]
Frequent open dumping and overflowing bins lead to rotting waste in public spaces. Food scraps, vegetable refuse, and general garbage are widespread on the ground.
3. Microbial Load on Shoe Soles
(bacteria, fungi, parasites)		 6/10[5]
Studies find high fecal indicator bacteria (e.g., E. coli, Enterococci) on NYC sidewalks, transferred to shoe bottoms. The city’s dog population contributes to microbial hotspots. 		10/10[6]
With open sewage and more frequent fecal contamination, Delhi shoes pick up dangerous pathogens (Salmonella, parasitic eggs, etc.) at extremely high levels.
4. Potential for Antibiotic-Resistant Bacteria 5/10[7]
MRSA and other resistant strains have been isolated in NYC’s subway and sidewalks. Risk is moderate, though city sanitation somewhat curbs widespread proliferation. 		8/10[8]
Antibiotic resistance is rising in India; street samples from Delhi show diverse drug-resistant microbes. Limited waste treatment amplifies the spread on surfaces.
5. Noxious Odors Sticking to Shoes 4/10[9]
Garbage smells are common in NYC, but the overall stench that clings to shoes is usually mild unless stepped in dog poop or leaked trash fluid. 		9/10[10]
Rotting waste, sewage, and manure are widespread, so shoes often retain foul odors. Dry conditions can embed odors in shoe materials.
6. Chemical and Toxic Residues
(pollutants, industrial runoff)		 6/10[11]
NYC streets can have traces of heavy metals (lead, etc.) and vehicle-related pollutants. De-icing salts in winter also leave chemical residues on soles. 		7/10[12]
Industrial discharge, construction dust, and vehicular pollutants collect on roadways, especially in congested areas. These can mix with standing wastewater.
7. Difficulty of Removing/ Cleaning Shoe Soles 3/10[13]
While grime can build up in NYC, regular wiping or rinsing typically handles the most common residues. Some areas have less sticky mud or filth. 		8/10[14]
Sticky organic muck, feces, and thick dust often get ground into shoe treads. Many Delhi residents remove shoes outside or dedicate footwear exclusively for outdoors.
8. Overall Shoe Contamination Risk (Aggregated Disgust Factor) 6/10[15]
NYC shoes pick up a fair amount of bacteria and minor organic residues. Unless one directly steps in feces or trash juice, contamination is moderate but still notable. 		10/10[16]
Almost certain to acquire serious filth—fecal matter, pathogens, and persistent odors—given the prevalence of waste and inadequate sanitation on Delhi streets.
